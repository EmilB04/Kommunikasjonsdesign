Day. November 2025

You've Already Met AI

Morning Routine

The alarm rings softly on your nightstand. You silence it with a tap, still half asleep and groggy. Your phone immediately presents you with the weather, suggests a new Spotify playlist, and reroutes your way to work route because there's a road work on your usual route. You check your email, the important ones have already been filterd as “important” before you even opened your inbox. You haven't said a single word yet, but artificial intelligence has been busy for hours, working on making your life more convenient.

The invisible assistant

AI isn't something futuristic anymore. It's everywhere, familiar, almost ordinary. We read about new breakthroughs and developments every week. Most of us already use them without a second thought. But what we rarely think about is the extent of it, how much of our daily life runs quietly on algorithms that anticipate what we need before we even ask? They decide what stories reach our screens, help teachers grade papers, assist doctors in diagnosis, and choose which songs keep us company while we cook.

Feeding the algorithm

Behind all this convenience is something a lot bigger then we might think, a system that quietly learns from us and develops with the information. Every choice we make feeds it. The songs we skip, the routes we take, the things we search for when we can't fall sleep. AI doesn't just follow instructions, it observes and reflects. Over time, it begins to understand our routines, our preferences, and maybe even our moods.
This story looks at that relationship, how artificial intelligence has become part of our daily life, shaping what we do and how we think, often more than we realize.

The Helpers We Don't See

Autopilot mode activated

Most of the time, we don't think about how many of our choices are quietly made by algorithms. We follow the blue line on the map without questioning it, trust the spellcheck, and appreciate the movie suggestions that appear when we open Netflix. It all feels natural and easy, like part of modern life. Few of us stop to wonder how much of that convenience depends on systems that are constantly running and learning from our actions and input.

From revolutionary to routine

Artificial intelligence has become the silent co-pilot of everyday life. As mentioned it corrects our typos, suggests what to watch, predicts traffic patterns, and can even help sort our photos by face or location. What once felt unimaginable and revolutionary has become routine. In our survey we found that over 70% of respondents use some form of Artificial Intelligence, and over half of them use it daily. These numbers show how deeply AI has settled into our daily life.

More then just convenience

For many people, AI has turned into a quiet form of assistance that saves time and removes small frustrations. It helps us find the fastest way home, choose a song that fits our mood, or translate a message in another language. For others, it can make a huge difference in accessibility and inclusion. Adaptive hearing aids use AI to separate speech from background noise, and smart home systems allow people with disabilities to get a good overview of their environment through voice assistant. These are small but powerful ways technology blends to fit human needs.

The panic with dependency

What makes this technology so effective is how invisible it feels. When it works, it feels so normal, when it fails, it reminds us of how dependent we have become on the technology. Most of us have experienced the quiet panic when Google Maps crashes or when translating a text message turns into something strange. These moments expose how much trust we place in systems we rarely think about.

Learning from us

AI systems are designed to learn and improve by learning from us. Every click, every search adds small pieces of information. Over time, the system builds a model of who we are. Not because it truly understands us, but because it recognizes patterns in our behavior. What it learns is not us personally, but our tendencies, our small habits that shows what gets our attention.
As Lance Eliot wrote in Forbes, "The AI is identifying a wide array of aspects about what you say, how you carry on the discussions, what interests you, what annoys you, and so on." He also warned that "you are actually and actively being profiled during your human-to-AI chats. Your every word and whatever you enter as prompts or otherwise opt to feed into the AI is getting sliced and diced by the AI." [1]

These observations highlight how closely AI systems watch and interpret our behavior, not to understand us as individuals, but to better predict our needs.
This quiet relationship between humans and algorithms is easy to overlook because it feels so natural. The more we depend on AI in daily life, the more it quietly shapes what we notice and what just slips past us. The helpers we don't notice keep learning from us, and in turn we grow used to trusting them, often blindly.

The Cost of Convenience

The hidden footprint of AI

Artificial intelligence feels effortless on the surface. Just a few clicks and you have the answer to your question, perfect, right? But behind the screen, it depends on huge computer centers that use a lot of electricity, water, and natural resources. Every time someone asks the AI a question or requests it to generate an image, thousands of servers start working at once. As you can imagine, this produces a lot of heat that needs to be cooled down.
According to the International Energy Agency (IEA), global electricity use from data centers, cryptocurrency, and AI could nearly double by 2030, reaching around 945 terawatt hours (TWh) per year - about the same amount of power that the entire country of Japan uses in a single year[2]. In 2024, data centers already consumed about 415 TWh, which is roughly 1.5% of all electricity used worldwide[3].

AI energy consumption

AI servers are growing particularly fast. The IEA estimates that power demand from AI-related machines could increase by 30% each year, while normal data grows by less than 10%. With these projections, by the end of this decade, AI could make up half of all data center energy use.

Many of these facilities are cooled using water systems. A Microsoft report showed that the company's global water usage increased by 34% in one year, 6.4 million cubic meters of water in 2022[4]. Google reported similar growth, especially in Oregon and Iowa, where local data centers rely heavily on community water sources[5]. In some U.S. towns, residents were even asked to reduce their own water and power use so nearby AI data centers could keep operating. Communities are starting to question whether these projects are worth it. They bring some investment and jobs, but they also drain local resources and raise utility prices. A few towns have even paused or limited new data center construction after public protests[6].

When images fool us

Another cost of AI is trust. Image and video generators have become so advanced that even experts struggle to tell what's real and what's fake. A 2022 study published in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences (PNAS) found that participants could no longer separate AI-generated faces from real ones consistently and often rated the fake faces as more trustworthy[7]. That was three years ago, and since then, AI tools have become far more capable.

Below are two images, one collected from Freepik.com (Figure 4) and one generated with ChatGPT. In our survey, most people guessed correctly, but around 12% chose wrong. While not being the largest percentage, it still shows how convincing AI-generated images can be. Can you tell which one is fake?

The ability to generate realistic images is both fascinating and scary. When fake visuals become impossible to detect, misinformation spreads fast and identity abuse becomes easier.

The harm behind the convenience

The same tools that create art and entertainment are also being used in far more harmful ways. The Internet Watch Foundation (IWF) reported a sharp increase in AI-generated child sexual abuse material[8]. In the first half of 2025, the organization found 210 webpages containing such content - a 400% increase compared to 2024. Those pages included 1,286 videos, up from only two the year before[9].
Many of these images and videos use real children's faces or bodies. The IWF warns that full-length AI-generated abuse videos are now becoming possible, describing them as "inevitable unless stricter safeguards are put in place"[10]. While major AI companies like OpenAI and Google have strict policies to block harmful content, smaller platforms often don't. Some even advertise “uncensored” generators that let users create fake nude or explicit images for a subscription fee. These tools are easy to access, hard to regulate, and cause real harm to people whose faces and bodies are used without their consent.

The trade-off

AI promises convenience, instant answers, personalized feeds, and realistically generated images. But it all comes at a cost most people don't see. The power needed to run these massive systems affects local communities as well as the planet. It makes truth harder to separate from fiction. And the same creative freedom that inspires us has also turned into a weapon for misinformation and abuse.
The technology is still developing, but in the future, it could be an even bigger threat for military use. This would raise new risks for global security, as AI could one day be used as a weapon of mass destruction and cause countless casualties.

AI at Work and School

At the office

A notification pops up on Sara's laptop "Grammarly has made 12 suggestions to improve your writing." She's halfway through a big quarterly report that was supposed to be in yesterday and at this point she's grateful for any help she can get. A few clicks later, her sentences are cleaner, her tone is more professional, and she's saved time not having to proofread.
Down the hall, her colleague Marcus is utilising AI in a different way. "Write me a first draft for the marketing campaign proposal, focus on sustainability and target millennials." is what he asks ChatGPT to help him with. Thirty seconds later, he has two pages of ideas to work with. They are not perfect or finished by any means, but it is a solid foundation that would have taken him hours to brainstorm himself.
This is how a day at the office is now. Not with dramatic robot takeovers or sci-fi scenarios, but with small assistance that slips into our daily routines with convenience. According to an article from NRK in 2025, more than half of Norwegians are already using AI to help with writing, research or other daily tasks. In the three months leading up to October 2025, 54% of people between 16 and 79 reported that they use AI. Compared to 2024, when it was only 36%, we can see a clear and significant increase. This shows how quickly the technology is becoming part of everyday life, both at work and at school.[11]. Adam Biddlecombe, founder of the AI newsletter Mindstream, made a post on LinkedIn saying "Stuck staring at a blank page? Ideas refusing to show up? Brain fried from overthinking? ChatGPT can spark it. Prompts can fuel it. Your creativity can explode."[12] His point being that AI can be used to enhance your creativity. Acting as a powerful partner that helps you power through mental blocks or generate fresh ideas.

In the classroom

But it is in schools where the real worries come out. Teachers nowadays are caught between embracing a tool that could revolutionize learning and worrying that it might replace the entire learning process for their students. Cherie Shields, a high school English teacher in Oregon says "We've already had a number of students turn AI-generated essays in and they're so easy to spot. It's flawless. Not a single grammatical error. All the sentences are perfect. And when you're working with students of that age, you know that they don't write flawlessly like that."[13]
The numbers tell the story of a fast developing and wildly used tool. A 2024 global survey by the DEC found that around 86% of students use generative AI in their school work, and more than half rely on it on a weekly bases[14]. Among U.S. high schoolers, a 2025 Pew Research Center report found that about one in four teens have used ChatGPT for schoolwork. This is double the amount from last year. These aren't necessarily students looking to cheat, many describe AI as a study partner, tutor, or brainstorming companion.
As one student explained from the University of Maryland's Generative AI Teaching and Learning Survey[15], Some students said that AI tools help them express themselves more clearly in a second language or understand complex concepts before class.

The Future Is Already Here

AI is changing the way we study and perform at work. In classrooms, it encourages students to think more critically instead of just memorizing information. In the office, it takes repetitive tasks off our plate and gives us more room for creative thinking. Whether that feels like progress or not really depends on how you look at it. But one thing's certain, this technology is already shaping our future, and it's here to stay.

The Future in Your Pocket

The phone that knows you personally

You might not even notice when it happens. One morning soon, your phone could do more than just answer your questions. It might quietly rearrange your day. If it senses you slept poorly, it could delay your first meeting, lower the brightness of your lights, and play a calming playlist before you have even had your coffee.

Reading between the lines and your emotions

This is the kind of future experts now refer to as ambient AI. It is technology that fades into the background and responds to context rather than commands. According to a MIT Technology Review from 2025, researchers and companies are now focusing on developing AI systems that can interpret surroundings and user behavior to work more intuitively[16].
Pieces of that future are already here. Smartwatches monitor heart rate, sleep, and stress. Earbuds can translate conversations in real time. Phones recognize patterns in how we move, type, and speak. Each small improvement quietly deepens the connection between people and machines. A study published in ScienceDirect (2023) explains how context-aware and ambient intelligence systems are learning to adapt to human activity continually through sensors and environmental feedback[17].
BBC News has reported that wearable AI can now detect emotional states and even identify signs of fatigue or anxiety[18]. This hints at a future where technology can respond to us not just logically but emotionally. These developments show how AI is slowly becoming less visible yet more integrated into our daily routines.

Big tech's involvement

Meanwhile, major technology companies are moving in the same direction. MIT Technology Review (2025) found that the next stage of AI development focuses on systems that understand context, work seamlessly across devices, and adapt to personal behavior in real time[16]. Industry leaders (like Apple or Google) are competing to build integrated, context-aware intelligence that does not simply respond to instructions but anticipates what people need next.

What it takes to anticipate emotions

It all sounds convenient and almost magical, until you think about what it requires. For AI to truly anticipate our needs, it has to know us well. Not just our preferences but our routines, our habits, and even our moods. The more this technology blends into the background, the easier it becomes to forget that it's constantly observing us and learning. This might be the most profound shift of all, because when technology stops asking and starts knowing, the question that remains is:

Will AI still feel invisible when it starts knowing you better than you do?

“Living With the Machine”

Seamless and unnoticeable

The day starts like any other. The alarm goes off, your phone lights up, and before you even say anything it has already started doing small things for you. It knows the weather, sets your route to work, and plays the right playlist to match your morning mood. None of this feels strange anymore. It's just how things are now. AI has become part of the background of our lives, quietly helping us without us even thinking about it.

Just an ordinary every day helper

Through this story, we've seen how deeply it has settled into everything we do. It fixes our spelling, gives us directions, writes drafts, sorts our photos, and predicts what we might need next. In our own survey, most people said they use AI at least once a day. The usage was mainly for productivity, speed and learning but also decision making, insight and brainstorming. These results alone shows just how ordinary AI has become in such a short time, and the broadness of its applications.

There will always be a cost

But this convenience comes with a cost. Behind the fast answers and smooth tools, there is the energy that keeps servers running, the people who moderate content, and the systems that quietly shape what we see online. At school and at work, AI can be a powerful helper, but it also raises new challenges. Some people use it to boost creativity or get inspiration, while others worry it makes us think less or rely too much on quick results. It reflects how we approach learning, time, and effort. Balancing between help and full blown dependence.

The Choice Is Ours

Now AI is moving beyond our screens into the spaces where we live. Smart devices learn our habits, voice assistants listen for our requests, and health apps track our well-being. The line between human choices and digital guidance is becoming thinner every year. AI doesn't feel futuristic anymore because it's already here, it's settled into our routines, shaping our decisions.
Living with AI isn't just about technology anymore. It's about how we adapt to it, how we stay aware of what it does for us, and what it learns from us in return. The more we understand that relationship, the better chance we have to make our own choices instead of just going along with whatever the technology decides for us.
